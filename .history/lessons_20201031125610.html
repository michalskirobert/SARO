Alphabet

Welcome in our first lession! Today we will learn how to read in polish and how to pronounce polish letters and words!

Polish alphabet is based by latin script and contains 32 letters. It's also known as [abecadło], although it is not the official name. The word "abecadło" is more famous for kids, because of Julian Tuwim's poem with the same title.

Pronunciation of the letters is not as hard, as you could imagine. A lot of the sounds are similiar to another sounds in japanese, chinese or korean. I will do my best, to show you the easiest way to read all of them.

Of course, certain words can be read in different ways than letter alone.

There are two groups in polish letters. One of them is called "samogłoski" what means, that you don't need any other symbol to read it, and the second one is called "spółgłoski" and it means, that it is almost impossible to say them without supporting letter from the first group.
Here is the e.g.
without supporting letter: b, p, t, d, s, h, k
with supporting letter: be, pe, te, de, sy, ha, ka

In Polish language we have some digraphs and trigraphs. It's important to know them too, even though they are not officialy the part of the alphabet:

Ch - read like H e.g. chałwa
Ci - read as Ć, but with longer i e.g. cisza
Cz - read as english ch e.g. czarny
Dz - read as something between short C and Z example dzban
Dzi - read as something between ci and ź e.g. dzik
Dź - read as the dzi, but shorter e.g. dźwięk
Ni - read as Ń, but longer e.g. nic
Rz - read as Ż e.g. rzeka
Si - read as Ś, but longer e.g. siano
Sz - read as english sh e.g. szok
Zi - read as Ź, but longer e.g. zima

Some words in polish are just adapted from the other language. Because of that, even if following letters are not in the official alphabet, we have our way to pronounce them:

X - [iks] - e.g. eks, ksylofon
Q - [ku] - e.g. kwartet, quad
V - [w] - e.g. wentylacja

One last thing I want to note in this lesson is ortography. I have shown you the some examples of words and letters, that has similiar or same reading. You probably wonder then, why are there words that are written in another way?

Polish language has the set of rules, which says how the word is written. Sometimes the meaning changes with the writing. For example, the word: morze means sea, when the word może means maybe - but they are pronounced the same way. I won't discuss the ortography here, but there will be another section only for these rules. But if you don't want to learn ortography, you can just remember the writing of the word.

Declension

Sometimes the Polish words you see look slightly different when used in different contexts - even in the simplest of sentences.
Take a look at an example:

Dom - house

Idę do domu. - I am going home. (lit. I go to house.)

Why is that? The Polish language has a feature called declension. In simple words, this means that the nouns change their forms depending on their role in the sentence - analogously to conjugation, which describes changing verbs.
Sometimes the form is dictated by the context, and sometimes - by the neighbouring prepositions and particles.
The different forms are sometimes called cases by linguists.

Declension is a very important part of the Polish language, but don’t worry if you get it wrong - even the Polish sometimes have major problems with choosing the right case!

Declension in Polish is called deklinacja or, more casually, odmiana przez przypadki (lit. change through cases). Przypadek in this context means a single case of a noun. Its plural form is przypadki.

Polish has 7 cases, and each of the forms has a general question associated with it:

Mianownik - kto? co?
Dopełniacz - kogo? czego?
Celownik - komu? czemu?
Biernik - kogo? co?
Narzędnik - z kim? Z czym?
Miejscownik - o kim? o czym?
Wołacz - o!

Now it’s time to introduce each of them one by one.

Mianownik:
The most basic form. Answers the question: kto? co? (basic who? what?), usually when naming things.

Co to jest?
To jest mój telefon.

What is this?
This is my phone.

Kto jest na tym zdjęciu?
Znajomy ze szkoły.
Who is in this photo?
A friend from school.

Dopełniacz:

Usually denotes a lack of something, something you need or want. Answers the question: kogo? Czego?

Czego nie wziąłeś?
Mojego telefonu.

What didn’t you take with you?
My phone.
Kogo dzisiaj nie ma?
Mojego znajomego.

Who is not here today?
My friend.

Celownik

Usually denotes the target of an action, e.g. when giving things to somebody. Answers the question: komu? Czemu?

Komu dajesz prezent?
Mamie.

To whom are you giving a gift?
To mum.

Czemu się przyglądasz?
Obrazowi na ścianie.

What are you looking at?
The painting on the wall.

Biernik

Also usually denotes the target of an action. Often used when doing something to an object - reading, eating, buying, etc. Answers the question: kogo? co?

Whether you should use biernik, or celownik, or other cases often depends on the preposition and verb. In general, biernik is used more often than celownik, so if you don’t know what to use, biernik is often a safe bet!
- Co czytasz?
Artykuł w wiadomościach.

- What are you reading?
An article from the news.

- Kogo zapraszasz?
Mojego chłopaka.

- Whom are you inviting?
My boyfriend.

Narzędnik

Usually used when an object serves as a tool to perform a task (hence the name - narzędzie means tool in Polish!). It is also used with certain prepositions (e.g. z - with, przed - before). Answers the question: z kim? z czym?, also kim? czym? in certain contexts.

- Czym jedziecie na wakacje?
Samochodem

Lit. By what are you going on holiday? (by what means of transport?)
By car.

- Z kim rozmawiasz?
Z żoną.
- With whom are you speaking?
With (my) wife.

Miejscownik

Frequently occurs with prepositions of places (hence the name - miejsce means place in Polish!), e.g. w - in, into, na - on, onto. Also used with the preposition o (about) to denote a topic. Answers the question: o kim? o czym?, also kim? czym? in certain contexts.

- O kim rozmawiasz?
O prezydencie.

- Who are you talking about?
About the president.

- Gdzie jest jedzenie?
Na stole.

- Where is the food?
On the table.

Wołacz

Used relatively seldom, in one kind of situation only - when addressing someone directly and wanting to emphasize this fact (e.g. in a large group of people), alternatively in literary speech.

Mamo! Przyjdź do mnie!
Mum! Come to me!

A ty co sądzisz, Moniko?
And what do you think, Monika?
(here Monika is the name and Moniko is its wołacz form)

Fortunately, that is all there is to Polish declension. It might seem like a lot, but when you’re just starting your journey with Polish, don’t overthink it. The most important tips for learning it are: Don’t just learn all the cases by heart - make sample sentences instead so that you understand the usage well.
Select particular situations that will be useful for you - e.g. a certain verb, a certain structure, and remember the cases for those situations only.
Do not attempt to learn all words you know in all cases - there are really a lot of them, and at least 50% will remain unused anyway.

In case you have wondered, here is a sample declension table which you might encounter during your study:

Mianownik - dom
Dopełniacz - domu
Celownik - domowi
Biernik - dom
Narzędnik - domem
Miejscownik - domu
Wołacz - domu

There are a lot of simple websites with declension, e.g. https://odmiana.net/. You can type the word and all 7 cases (both singular and plural) will appear.

Good luck, and don’t hesitate to come back here after having learned new words and usages!

Word order

The most important thing when speaking a language is making sentences. You can learn as much vocabulary as you want, but the ability to put it together is what really showcases your skills. So - in this lesson we are going to take a quick look at forming basic sentences in Polish.
Firstly, the overall order of the sentence is:

subject + verb + object (SVO).

What does this mean? First we say who did something, then - what action they did, then - with whom / using which tools / where etc. It’s usually the same as in English.


For instance:

Monika czyta książkę. - Monika is reading a book (lit. Monika reads book.)
Moja mama pracuje w banku. - My mum works in a bank.
James nie mówi po polsku. - James does not speak Polish (lit. James not speaks Polish.)
Sometimes the order or additional word might change slightly depending on the emphasis. Usually the last thing is emphasised the most, e.g.


Pierogi gotuję bardzo często. (I cook pierogi very often!)
Bardzo często gotuję pierogi. (I often cook pierogi compared to other food.)

Usually this does not really matter, in complicated sentences 1 sentence has more than 1 correct order. Just remember that the subject comes before the verb!
An important exception are words denoting time - they often come at the very beginning.
Jutro nie idę do pracy - I am not going to work tomorrow (lit. Tomorrow I not go to work.)
Teraz mieszkam w Krakowie. - I now live in Cracow.
Another important thing to note is that negation is achieved just by adding nie - no - before the verb.
Because Polish verbs must be conjugated, it is actually possible to guess the subject without using any words for the subjects. Unlike in English, we usually omit the subject if it was introduced before, both when speaking and writing (it generally sounds unnatural to leave it).

For example:

I am reading a book. ->

Ja czytam książkę.

->

Czytam książkę.

I don’t have any siblings. -> Ja nie mam rodzeństwa. - > Nie mam rodzeństwa.
She is eating an apple. -> Ona je jabłko. -> Je jabłko. (if subject already known from context)
They are going home. -> Oni idą do domu. -> Idą do domu. (if subject already known from context)

Questions:

Luckily, no complicated rules here. We ask yes/no questions just by adding czy at the beginning of a sentence, or informally - raising our voices.
Czy mówisz po polsku? / Mówisz po polsku? - Do you speak Polish?
Czy czytasz książkę? / (better) Czytasz książkę? - Are you reading a book?
Czy ona ma rodzeństwo? (formally) / Ma rodzeństwo? (in a casual conversation with known context) - Does she have any siblings?

Take a look at this text. Every sentence in it is incorrect or unnatural - try and change the order or words so that it sounds better!
Ja nazywam się Mariko. Ja jestem z Japonii. Mieszkam w Warszawie teraz. W małej szkole ja pracuję i ja uczę języka japońskiego. Mieszkałam w Tokio i w Londynie kiedyś. Warszawa jest nie bardzo duża. Mimo tego to miasto lubię. Ono ma dużo parków i starych budynków. Do Warszawy moi znajomi przyjadą niedługo. My na wycieczkę po Warszawie pójdziemy.


Solution:

Nazywam się Mariko. Jestem z Japoniii. Teraz mieszkam w Warszawie. Pracuję w małej szkole i uczę języka japońskiego. Kiedyś mieszkałam w Tokio i w Londynie. Warszawa nie jest bardzo duża. Mimo tego lubię to miasto. Ma dużo parków i starych budynków. Niedługo moi znajomi przyjadą do Warszawy. Pójdziemy na wycieczkę po Warszawie.
Translation: My name is Mariko. I come from Japan. Now I live in Warsaw. I am working in a small school and teaching Japanese. Earlier I have been living in Tokyo and in London. Warsaw isn’t very big. Despite that, I like this city. It has a lot of parks and old buildings. Soon my friends will come. We will go on a trip around Warsaw.


Vocabulary

Teraz - now
Mieszkać (-am, asz) - to live (in a flat, house, city...)
Pracować (-ę, -esz) - to work
Mały - small
Szkoła - school
Uczyć (-ę, -ysz) - to teach
Język japoński - Japanese language
Kiedyś - earlier, once
Bardzo - very
Duży - big
Mimo tego - despite that Lubić (-ę, -isz) - to like Miasto - city Park - park Stary - old Budynek (l. mn. budynki) - building
Niedługo - soon
Pójść (-ę, -esz) - to go
Wycieczka - trip
Wycieczka po Warszawie - a trip around Warsaw

Adjectives

As you could see in the previous lesson about declension, in Polish language nouns change depending on so called cases (pl. przypadki). Adjectives don’t differ in this aspect. There are 7 cases, and the questions we ask to find a right change (odmiana) are unchangeable.
Adjective’s function is to describe a noun so it answers questions: what (kind of)?/ which? ( pl. jaki, jaka, jakie?), which (one)? (który, która, które?), whose? (czyj, czyja, czyje?).

Adjective is a changeable part of speech. It changes according to the same rules as a noun - through cases, numbers and genders. To know what ending we should add in the end of an adjective to modify it correctly, we need to know: what is the case, grammatical gender of a noun we are describing and if the noun is in the plural or in the singular.
Almost all the Polish adjectives change according to one regular scheme, which is as follows:

pink the basic form of adjective (feminine Nominative)
green (endings with e):
- the second form (with i) in the case of adjectives which ends on: ka (e.g. wielka), ga (e.g. długa), ia (e.g. głupia) in feminine Nominative;
- in other cases: the form without i;
yellow (endings with i / y):
- the second form (with i) in the case of adjectives which ends on: ka (e.g. wielka), ga (e.g. długa), la (e.g. ośla), ia (e.g. głupia) in feminine Nominative;
- in other cases: the form without i;
blue - the second form (with i) in the case of adjectives which ends on ia (e.g. głupia) in feminine Nominative;
- in other cases: the form without i ;
red it is needed to soften the sound which comes directly before by adding i or y;
NOTICE : instead of endings -szy and sometimes -ży ( not -rzy), phonetically softened -si, -zi can appeared.

Noun grammatical gender

At the very early stage of learning Polish, you might discover how different a man and a woman will introduce themselves. Let me provide you two examples, see below.

Damian:
Cześć! Jestem Damian, mam 28 lat. Urodziłem się w Polsce, jestem Polakiem. Pracuję jako nauczyciel w szkole.

Anna:
Cześć! Jestem Anna, mam 27 lat. Urodziłam się w Polsce, jestem Polką. Pracuję jako nauczycielka w szkole.


And here are the translations:

Damian:
Hello! I am Damian, I am 28 years old. I was born in Poland, I am Polish. I work as a teacher at school.

Anna:
Hello! I am Anna, I am 27 years old. I was born in Poland, I am Polish. I work as a teacher at school.

Apart from declension, a vital role in sentence-forming is played by gender. In case of the examples above, the differences come from the fact that each speaker is of different gender. As you can see, in these short bits there are three places (red) formed in accordance with the speaker’s gender.
I believe now you can see what an important grammar aspect is gender. Now let’s move on and discover more regarding noun gender in Polish.
In Polish language, every noun has got a gender. Three basic genders are:

    • masculine (rodzaj męski)
      feminine (rodzaj żeński)
      neuter (rodzaj nijaki)

    • Since we started with people, let’s continue with this. It is easy to distinguish gender of nouns pointing to people:
    Ten mężczyzna – this man -> masculine
    Ten chłopiec – this boy -> masculine
    Ta kobieta – this woman -> feminine
    Ta dziewczyna – this girl -> feminine
    To dziecko – this child -> neuter
    Quite easy, right?

    Now, the challenge starts with learning nouns related to things and to animals. In case of those, grammatical gender has got nothing to do with an actual gender – I mean how do you know what gender a window or an apple is? Truth be told, these need to be learnt by heart. A tip – when studying nouns always learn them together with a gender!

    Here are some examples of masculine nouns:

    dom – house ten dom – this house
    samochód – car ten samochód - this car
    pies - dog ten pies - this dog
    kot - cat ten kot - this cat
    deszcz - rain ten deszcz- this rain
    las - forest ten las - this forest
    długopis - pen ten długopis - this pen

    Here are some examples of feminine nouns:

    kamera – video recorder ta kamera – this video recorder
    gitara – guitar ta gitara - this guitar
    szkoła - school ta szkoła - this school
    butelka - bottle ta butelka - this bottle
    herbata - tea ta herbata - this tea
    kawa - coffee ta kawa - this cofee
    krowa - cow ta krowa - this cow

    In general nouns ending with -a are feminine but there are some exceptions, for example sprzedawca – shop assistant (masculine) or kierowca – driver (masculine).

    There are also rare cases in which feminine noun does not end with -a, like sól – salt (feminine).


    Here are some examples of neuter nouns:

    okno - window to okno - this window
    oko - eye to oko - this eye
    bagno - swamp to bagno - this swamp
    piwo - beer to piwo - this beer
    auto - car to auto - this car
    ciasto - cake to ciasto - this cake
    kakao - cocoa to kakao - this cocoa

    As you may have noticed, neuter nouns usually end with -o.

    That’s the basic basics really Don’t worry if you have trouble with this at first, you will get it in time. It will be good if you make habit of remembering nouns along with their grammatical gender.
